üéØ What is Reality Check?

Reality Check is a satirical multiplayer board game that parodies modern adult life. Set in the fictional town of Babel, players navigate the chaos of careers, relationships, health, and personal growth while making moral choices that shape their destiny.

üåÄ The Chaos Circle Board

The game board is a mathematically-generated hypocycloid with five concentric rings representing different aspects of life:

üî¥ Career Ring

Jobs, promotions, corporate drama, unemployment, side hustles, and the endless grind of professional life.

üü° Health Ring

Physical and mental wellbeing, therapy, medical bills, gym memberships you never use, and existential crises.

üîµ Social Ring

Relationships, family drama, dating apps, social media scandals, and the complex web of human connections.

üü¢ Personal Growth Ring

Hobbies, spirituality, self-improvement, creative pursuits, and the eternal quest for meaning.

üü£ Babel Events Ring

Town-wide chaos that affects everyone: market crashes, natural disasters, political upheaval, and collective madness.

üìä Player Stats

Your character is tracked through four core statistics that fluctuate throughout the game:

üí∞ Money

Your financial situation. Can be positive (wealth) or negative (debt). Affects available choices and life quality.

üß† Mental Health

Your psychological wellbeing. Low mental health can limit options and make you vulnerable to bad decisions.

üòà Sin

Your moral corruption level. High sin opens dark opportunities but comes with consequences at judgment.

üòá Virtue

Your moral goodness level. High virtue provides protection and noble opportunities, but virtue has costs.

üÉè Card Types

Sin Cards (Red)

Temptations with immediate benefits but moral costs. Examples: embezzlement, fraud, betrayal, cryptocurrency scams, and MLM schemes.

Virtue Cards (Blue)

Opportunities for sacrifice and moral growth. Examples: charity, therapy, helping others, environmental activism, and forgiveness.

Chaos Cards (Purple)

Random life events that test adaptability. Examples: surprise inheritance, job loss, medical emergencies, and natural disasters.

üí° Card Strategy: Many cards have delayed effects that trigger later in the game. Your choices early on can come back to haunt or help you turns later.

‚öñÔ∏è Moral Complexity

Reality Check doesn't have simple good vs. evil choices. The game explores:

  • Gray Area Decisions: Reporting tax evasion vs. loyalty to friends
  • Contextual Morality: Stealing when bankrupt vs. stealing when wealthy
  • Unintended Consequences: Good intentions leading to bad outcomes
  • Systemic Issues: How individual choices interact with broken systems
  • Temporal Ethics: How past virtues can be tested by future circumstances

üé® Design Philosophy

"We have to laugh at life in order to see it as it truly is."

Reality Check uses humor and absurdity to mirror real-world systems, exploring:

  • The illusion of meritocracy in modern capitalism
  • How wealth affects empathy and social connections
  • The randomness of birth circumstances vs. personal choices
  • The impact of social media on perception and reality
  • The beautiful chaos of trying to be a good person in a complicated world
